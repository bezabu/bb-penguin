Who are the penguins?

Depending on who you ask, there are between 17 and 20 living species of penguin, ranging from the Little penguin, which can grow up to 33cm and is found in Australia and New Zealand, to the tallest and heaviest Emperor penguin which inhabits the coldest reaches of Antarctica.

  • Great penguins (Emperor penguin, King penguin)
  • Brush-Tailed penguins (Adélie penguin, Gentoo penguin, Chinstrap penguin)
  • Little penguins (Australian Little penguin, Little Blue penguin)
  • Banded penguins (Magellanic penguin, Humboldt penguin, Galápagos penguin, African penguin)
  • Megadyptes (Yellow-eyed penguin, Waitaha penguin)
  • Crested penguins (Fiordland penguin, Snares penguin, Erect-crested penguin, southern and northern rockhopper penguins, Royal penguin, Macaroni penguin)

Where are the penguins?

Penguins are native to the southern hemisphere, but they are not limited to Antarctica. Some species may be found as far north as Angola, Argentina, Australia and even the Galápagos Islands. Generally, the larger species are found in Antarctica while the smaller species live further north in warmer climates.

Penguins are flightless birds, although they swim so well that below the surface of the water they look like birds in flight. Penguins tend to hunt for food in the cold waters surrounding Antarctica and breed on land (or ice). Gentoo penguins are the fastest swimmers, capable of reaching speeds of up to 36km (22 miles) per hour while searching for food or evading predators.

What are Emperor penguins?

The Emperor Penguin is the tallest and heaviest of the penguins. They reach around 115cm in height and weigh up to 45kg. Apart from their size, the easiest way to tell Emperor penguins from the similar looking King penguins is the colour of their ear patches. In emperor penguins the feathers are white with a hint of orange which fades to black along the back of the head, whereas in king penguins the feathers are orange and contained in a little circle.

Emperor penguin's diet primarily consists of fish but also includes crustaceans, such as krill, and cephalapods such as squid. The lifespan of a typical emperor penguin is 20 years in the wild, although observations suggest that some individuals may live to 50 years of age.

Emperor colonies are only found in the Antarctic, where they breed on frozen sea ice. Because of this they may never set foot on land. Males are left in charge of the egg, keeping it warm in it's brood pouch (a warm pouch of skin) while balancing it on top of his feet. He does this for around 70 days while the mother looks for food.

What are Adélie penguins?

Adélie penguins sport a characteristic black and white ‘tuxedo’ look, with white rings around their eyes. Male and female Adélie penguins look very similar which can make it hard to distinguish them. They are only found along the coast of the Antarctic continent. They are the most widespread penguin species and is the most southerly distributed, along with the Emperor penguin.

The Adélie penguin was named by the French Antarctic explorer Jules Dumont d'Urville, who named them after his wife Adéle. They were noted by early explorers to be quite curious and unafraid of humans and their dogs. They have also been known to take on predators such as seals or large seabirds.

Males attempt to attract potential mates by building the biggest and best nests out of small rocks and stones. Sometimes unscrupulous males will steal building materials from neighbouring nests.

Adélie penguins are impressive swimmers, they need to be to escape from Leopard Seals. Adélie penguins feed mainly on krill, but scientists now believe that this is a fairly recent preference following the decline of the Antarctic Fur Seal and Baleen Whales, both of which also feed on krill.

Despite this, krill populations are under threat from overfishing by humans and climate change threatens the loss of Antarctic ice sheets where they breed.

What's happening to the penguins?

Thanks to the effects of climate change and industrial overfishing, the majority of penguin species have declining populations. The IUCN classifies 5 species of penguin as vulnerable and 5 species as endangered. There 5 species of penguin on the endangered list:

  • Northern Rockhopper Penguin
  • Erect-Crested Penguin
  • Yellow-Eyed Penguin
  • African Penguin
  • Galápagos Penguin

In 2012 the largest and most well known species of penguin, the Emperor Penguin was uplisted from a species of least concern to near threatened by the IUCN.

According to NASA, Antarctica is losing ice mass at an average rate of 150 billion tons per year. The West Antarctic Ice Sheet in particular has warmed by 2.4°C since 1958. Emperor and Adélie penguins depend on sea ice for their main food source.
