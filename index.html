Who are the penguins?

Depending on who you ask, there are between 17 and 20 living species of penguin, ranging from the Little penguin, which can grow up to 33cm and is found in Australia and New Zealand, to the tallest and heaviest Emperor penguin which inhabits the coldest reaches of Antarctica.

  • Great penguins (Emperor penguin, King penguin)
  • Brush-Tailed penguins (Adélie penguin, Gentoo penguin, Chinstrap penguin)
  • Little penguins (Australian Little penguin, Little Blue penguin)
  • Banded penguins (Magellanic penguin, Humboldt penguin, Galápagos penguin, African penguin)
  • Megadyptes (Yellow-eyed penguin, Waitaha penguin)
  • Crested penguins (Fiordland penguin, Snares penguin, Erect-crested penguin, southern and northern rockhopper penguins, Royal penguin, Macaroni penguin)

Where are the penguins?

Penguins are native to the southern hemisphere, but they are not limited to Antarctica. Some species may be found as far north as Angola, Argentina, Australia and even the Galápagos Islands.

What are Emperor penguins?

The Emperor Penguin is the tallest and heaviest of the penguins. They reach around 115cm in height and weigh up to 45kg. Apart from their size, the easiest way to tell Emperor penguins from the similar looking King penguins is the colour of their ear patches. In emperor penguins the feathers are white with a hint of orange which fades to black along the back of the head, whereas in king penguins the feathers are orange and contained in a little circle.

Emperor penguin's diet primarily consists of fish but also includes crustaceans, such as krill, and cephalapods such as squid. The lifespan of a typical emperor penguin is 20 years in the wild, although observations suggest that some individuals may live to 50 years of age.

Emperor colonies are only found in the Antarctic, where they breed on frozen sea ice. Because of this they may never set foot on land. Males are left in charge of the egg, keeping it warm in it's brood pouch (a warm pouch of skin) while balancing it on top of his feet. He does this for around 70 days while the mother looks for food.

What's happening to the penguins?

Thanks to the effects of climate change and industrial overfishing, the majority of penguin species have declining populations. The IUCN classifies 5 species of penguin as vulnerable and 5 species as endangered. There 5 species of penguin on the endangered list include:

  • Northern Rockhopper Penguin
  • Erect-Crested Penguin
  • Yellow-Eyed Penguin
  • African Penguin
  • Galápagos Penguin

In 2012 the largest and most well known species of penguin, the Emperor Penguin was uplisted from a species of least concern to near threatened by the IUCN.
